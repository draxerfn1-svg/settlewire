Bloomberg: Prediction Markets Function Effectively Without Real-Money Stakes

Published JUL 08, 2026 · 09:00 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

A Bloomberg opinion piece argues that prediction markets can generate accurate forecasts even when no actual money is wagered, challenging the assumption that financial incentives are essential to their reliability.

Bloomberg published a commentary asserting that real-money stakes are not a prerequisite for prediction markets to produce useful, accurate probability estimates. The piece points to evidence from play-money platforms and internal corporate forecasting tools that have demonstrated meaningful predictive accuracy without financial skin in the game.

The argument carries weight at a moment when platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket are expanding real-money event contracts in the United States, and regulators at the CFTC are actively scrutinizing whether financial incentives serve a legitimate economic purpose. Proponents of play-money markets contend that participation volume and reputational incentives can substitute for cash stakes.

Historically, platforms such as Iowa Electronic Markets and academic forecasting tournaments have shown that non-monetary prediction environments can rival or outperform traditional polling. The Bloomberg piece adds to an ongoing debate about whether the legal and regulatory burden of real-money contracts is justified by a meaningful accuracy premium over their no-stakes counterparts.

Why this matters

If play-money markets are empirically as accurate as real-money ones, regulators at the CFTC gain ammunition to restrict or block expansion of financial event contracts without appearing to suppress useful price discovery. For Kalshi and Polymarket, this narrative is a direct threat to the 'economic purpose' justification that underpins their regulatory approvals. Prediction-market traders should watch whether this argument surfaces in upcoming CFTC rulemaking comments or court filings.
