Gibraltar launches world's first dedicated prediction markets regulatory framework

Published JUL 13, 2026 · 09:30 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Gibraltar became the first jurisdiction globally to establish a standalone regulatory regime exclusively for prediction markets, creating a bespoke licensing framework separate from existing gambling or financial-services rules.

According to SBC News, Gibraltar has enacted what authorities describe as the world's first regulatory regime designed exclusively for prediction markets, distinguishing the territory's approach from jurisdictions that shoehorn event contracts into gambling statutes or derivatives frameworks. The move positions Gibraltar as a potential hub for operators seeking legal clarity.

The new framework reportedly establishes dedicated licensing categories and conduct standards tailored to prediction-market mechanics — including continuous trading, crowd-sourced probability pricing, and event-resolution procedures — rather than adapting legacy rules written for sports books or commodity exchanges. Specific fee structures and capitalization requirements were not publicly detailed in available coverage.

The development arrives as operators like Kalshi and Polymarket face fragmented oversight globally, with the CFTC asserting jurisdiction in the United States and various EU member states applying inconsistent gambling or MiFID II standards. A purpose-built offshore regime could attract platforms seeking regulatory certainty while U.S. and European frameworks remain unsettled.

Why this matters

A dedicated Gibraltar license gives prediction-market operators a credible, named jurisdiction to cite when negotiating with payment processors and institutional liquidity providers — a concrete commercial advantage. Traders on platforms that relocate or dual-license under Gibraltar rules may see expanded contract offerings previously blocked by U.S. or EU restrictions. Regulation watchers should monitor whether this framework influences CFTC rulemaking by demonstrating that standalone prediction-market oversight is administratively viable.
