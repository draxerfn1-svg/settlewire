World Cup prediction-market contracts may carry lower tax burden than sportsbook bets

Published JUL 12, 2026 · 12:00 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Bettors placing World Cup wagers on regulated prediction markets like Kalshi could face more favorable U.S. tax treatment than traditional sportsbook gamblers, according to a Yahoo Finance analysis published in 2025.

According to Yahoo Finance, winnings from prediction-market event contracts may be classified differently from traditional gambling income under U.S. tax law, potentially allowing traders to offset losses against gains more efficiently. Traditional sportsbook winnings are typically reported as 'other income,' with loss deductions limited to the amount won and subject to itemization rules that many filers cannot use.

Prediction-market contracts traded on CFTC-regulated platforms like Kalshi are structured as financial instruments rather than wagers, which could mean they fall under capital-gains or Section 1256 contract rules. Section 1256 contracts receive a blended 60/40 long-term/short-term capital-gains rate and allow losses to be carried back three years — a significant structural advantage over standard gambling loss treatment.

The distinction matters practically as the 2026 FIFA World Cup approaches and platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket are expected to list an array of outcome contracts on the tournament. Tax professionals quoted by Yahoo Finance cautioned that the IRS has not issued explicit guidance on prediction-market contract classification, leaving some legal ambiguity that traders should discuss with advisers before assuming favorable treatment.

Why this matters

If prediction-market contracts are confirmed to receive Section 1256 or capital-gains treatment, platforms gain a concrete marketing differentiator over sportsbooks that could accelerate user acquisition ahead of major sporting events. Traders who position heavily on World Cup markets should document their activity now, before IRS guidance potentially closes the gap. Regulators watching Kalshi's post-court-victory expansion will likely face pressure to clarify tax status as contract volume grows.
