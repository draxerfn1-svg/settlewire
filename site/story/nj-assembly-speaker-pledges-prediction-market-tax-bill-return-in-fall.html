NJ Assembly Speaker Pledges Prediction Market Tax Bill Return in Fall

Published JUL 07, 2026 · 11:04 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

New Jersey Assembly Speaker Craig Coughlin confirmed a prediction market taxation proposal will be reintroduced when the legislature reconvenes this fall, after the measure stalled earlier in 2025.

According to the New Jersey Monitor, Assembly Speaker Craig Coughlin indicated the prediction market tax legislation will come back before the chamber when the fall session begins. The bill had previously failed to advance, but Coughlin's public commitment signals renewed legislative momentum in one of the country's most active regulatory environments for gambling-adjacent products.

New Jersey's interest in taxing prediction markets reflects growing state-level attention to platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket, which have expanded aggressively since federal regulators cleared certain event contracts. State officials have been debating whether prediction markets constitute a taxable activity similar to sports wagering, which generates hundreds of millions in annual tax revenue for New Jersey.

The proposal's return in fall sets up a potential inflection point: if New Jersey enacts a tax framework, it could prompt other states to follow with their own regimes. The timing coincides with broader federal CFTC rulemaking discussions around event contracts, meaning operators could face a layered state-and-federal compliance landscape by late 2025 or early 2026.

Why this matters

A New Jersey tax law would be the first state-level fiscal framework specifically targeting prediction markets, creating a compliance cost precedent that traders should price into operator valuations. If enacted, it could suppress retail participation in NJ and trigger copycat legislation across other high-population states. Prediction-market operators without lobbyists or legal infrastructure to navigate state-by-state regimes face meaningful structural disadvantage.
