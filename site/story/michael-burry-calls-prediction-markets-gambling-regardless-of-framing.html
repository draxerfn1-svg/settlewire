Michael Burry calls prediction markets gambling, regardless of framing

Published JUL 13, 2026 · 15:07 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Michael Burry, the investor famous for predicting the 2008 housing collapse, publicly dismissed prediction markets as gambling in name only, according to Business Insider.

Burry, whose 2008 subprime mortgage short was immortalized in 'The Big Short,' leveled sharp criticism at prediction markets, arguing that rebranding speculative event contracts does not change their fundamental nature as gambling, according to Business Insider. His comments arrive as platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket are enjoying a high-profile surge in user activity and media attention following the 2024 U.S. election cycle.

The critique echoes longstanding regulatory skepticism from bodies like the CFTC, which has repeatedly debated whether event contracts serve a legitimate hedging or price-discovery function or simply attract retail speculators. Burry's public rebuke adds a prominent Wall Street voice to that camp, potentially amplifying pressure on platforms seeking broader regulatory acceptance and mainstream credibility.

Prediction market operators have argued their products generate genuine information value — aggregating crowd forecasts on elections, economic indicators, and geopolitical events. Burry's framing directly challenges that distinction, positioning the 'market' label as cosmetic rather than structural, a line of argument regulators and legislators have also deployed in past CFTC rulemaking proceedings.

Why this matters

A high-credibility critic like Burry gives legislators and regulators a quotable soundbite that could slow CFTC approval processes for new event-contract categories. Traders on platforms like Kalshi should watch for sentiment shifts among institutional participants who may cite reputational risk. Second-order, this could reduce retail inflows if mainstream media amplifies the 'it's just gambling' narrative heading into fresh regulatory review cycles.
