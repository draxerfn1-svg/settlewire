Goldman, firms grapple with insider trading rules as prediction markets grow

Published JUL 09, 2026 · 18:52 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Goldman Sachs and other major companies are actively developing internal policies to address insider trading risks posed by employee participation in prediction markets, according to CNBC.

Goldman Sachs and several unnamed peer firms are crafting compliance guardrails specifically targeting prediction markets, according to CNBC, as platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket attract growing retail and institutional participation. The concern centers on employees who hold material non-public information — about mergers, earnings, or policy decisions — and could theoretically exploit that edge by trading event contracts.

Unlike traditional securities, prediction markets occupy a regulatory gray zone: CFTC-regulated platforms such as Kalshi list event contracts, but existing insider trading statutes written for equities and commodities do not map cleanly onto binary outcome markets. Legal experts quoted by CNBC warn that the absence of explicit rules creates liability uncertainty for both employers and employees who trade these instruments.

Companies are reportedly considering blanket prohibitions on prediction-market trading for certain employee classes, mirroring restrictions already applied to individual stock trading. The ad-hoc patchwork of firm-level rules underscores the broader absence of a unified federal framework governing who can trade event contracts and on what information — a gap regulators have yet to formally close.

Why this matters

Compliance crackdowns at major financial institutions could significantly constrain the sophisticated-trader liquidity that prediction markets need to produce accurate prices. If firms prohibit employee participation, platforms lose a key source of informed order flow, which directly affects contract calibration and market depth. Separately, growing corporate anxiety over insider trading liability will likely accelerate lobbying pressure on the CFTC to issue explicit guidance on event-contract trading rules.
