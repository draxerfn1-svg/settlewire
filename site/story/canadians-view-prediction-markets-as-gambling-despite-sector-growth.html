Canadians View Prediction Markets as Gambling Despite Sector Growth

Published JUL 10, 2026 · 09:30 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

A Yahoo! Finance Canada report finds that most Canadians still categorize prediction markets as gambling, even as platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi expand globally in 2025.

According to Yahoo! Finance Canada, prediction markets are experiencing measurable growth in user participation and contract volume, yet Canadian public perception remains a significant barrier to mainstream adoption. The outlet highlights a persistent gap between how industry insiders frame these platforms — as forecasting and information-aggregation tools — and how the general Canadian public understands them.

The regulatory environment in Canada compounds the perception problem. Unlike the United States, where the CFTC has begun formally engaging with event-contract platforms such as Kalshi and ForecastEx, Canada lacks a unified federal framework for classifying or overseeing prediction markets, leaving them in a legal grey zone that reinforces the gambling label among consumers and policymakers alike.

Globally, the prediction-market sector has drawn renewed attention following high-volume political contract trading during the 2024 U.S. election cycle, with Polymarket alone recording hundreds of millions of dollars in open interest. That momentum has yet to translate into clear regulatory legitimacy or cultural acceptance in Canada, where provincial gaming authorities retain significant influence over how speculative contracts are classified.

Why this matters

Canadian regulatory ambiguity creates a two-sided risk for prediction-market operators eyeing northward expansion: without a CFTC-style engagement process, platforms face provincial patchwork enforcement that could freeze market access overnight. For traders, low Canadian retail participation means thinner liquidity on Canada-specific political and economic contracts, distorting prices. Regulators watching cross-border spillover should note that unresolved classification in major G7 economies can fragment global prediction-market liquidity and slow the sector's credibility gains.
