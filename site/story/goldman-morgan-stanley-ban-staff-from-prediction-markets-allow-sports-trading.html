Goldman, Morgan Stanley ban staff from prediction markets, allow sports trading

Published JUL 10, 2026 · 02:26 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley have prohibited employees from trading on prediction markets such as Kalshi and Polymarket while permitting sports-betting activity, according to Casino.org.

Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley issued internal policies barring staff from participating in financial prediction markets, including platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket, according to Casino.org. The distinction drawn by both firms is notable: sports wagering remains permissible for employees, while event contracts tied to economic or political outcomes are off-limits.

The banks' concern appears centered on conflicts of interest — traders and analysts at major financial institutions may possess material non-public information that could be exploited on prediction markets covering interest rates, elections, or macroeconomic events. Sports outcomes, by contrast, are seen as sufficiently removed from the firms' core business activities to pose less compliance risk.

The bans arrive as prediction markets are experiencing rapid mainstream growth, with Kalshi recently expanding into sports event contracts and Polymarket recording hundreds of millions in monthly volume. Compliance departments at large banks are now scrambling to write policies for an asset class that regulators and employers alike are still defining.

Why this matters

Bank-level employee bans signal that prediction markets are now viewed as material enough to trigger institutional compliance regimes — a strong indicator of the sector's growing legitimacy. For Kalshi and Polymarket, losing access to a cohort of sophisticated, high-volume traders could reduce liquidity depth in complex financial event markets. Regulators watching how major institutions self-police here may use these internal policies as informal benchmarks when drafting formal CFTC guidance on participant eligibility.
