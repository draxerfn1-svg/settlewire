Kalshi appeals NY court loss blocking sports event-contract state enforcement

Published JUL 08, 2026 · 10:37 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Kalshi filed a same-day appeal to the Second Circuit on Tuesday, July 8, 2026, after SDNY Judge Analisa Torres denied its bid to stop New York State Gaming Commission officials from enforcing state gambling laws against its sports event contracts.

Judge Torres ruled that Kalshi failed to meet the 'clear or substantial showing' standard required for a preliminary injunction, finding that the Commodity Exchange Act does not preempt New York's gambling statutes as applied to the platform's sports-related event contracts. The filing was made in the US District Court for the Southern District of New York, with Kalshi immediately escalating the dispute to the US Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit on the same day.

The ruling deepens a nationwide legal split over whether sports prediction-market contracts are federally regulated derivatives under CFTC jurisdiction or gambling products subject to state oversight. According to Cointelegraph, some courts have granted Kalshi injunctions against state enforcement while others, including now New York, have denied them — fragmenting the legal landscape across multiple jurisdictions simultaneously.

Gaming attorney Daniel Wallach, whose Florida firm specializes in sports wagering law, described the ruling as a 'major loss for Kalshi in the nation's financial capital,' warning of likely knock-on effects in related cases in Connecticut and other pending SDNY lawsuits, per Cointelegraph. The Second Circuit appeal now sets up a potentially precedent-setting federal appellate ruling on the federal-versus-state jurisdiction question.

Why this matters

A Second Circuit ruling against Kalshi could embolden additional states to enforce gambling laws against prediction-market operators before any federal appellate consensus emerges, creating immediate compliance risk for traders and platforms alike. Prediction-market traders pricing Kalshi's legal outcome should note that the Connecticut and other SDNY cases Wallach flagged may shift odds downward for a near-term pro-Kalshi resolution. Regulators watching this case will use the Second Circuit's eventual reasoning as a blueprint for whether CFTC preemption arguments can survive appellate scrutiny — a question with industry-wide implications.
