Polymarket launches margin trading, targeting Hyperliquid liquidity

Published JUL 10, 2026 · 11:46 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Polymarket introduced margin trading to its prediction-market platform, a structural upgrade that could draw leveraged liquidity away from decentralized derivatives venue Hyperliquid.

Polymarket's addition of margin trading marks a significant product expansion for the world's largest prediction-market platform, according to Yahoo Finance coverage. By allowing users to take leveraged positions on event contracts, Polymarket is no longer competing solely within the prediction-market niche — it is now encroaching on territory occupied by on-chain perps platforms like Hyperliquid.

Hyperliquid has attracted substantial decentralized-finance liquidity through its perpetual-futures model, but margin-enabled event contracts could offer traders a comparable leverage mechanism with real-world event exposure as the underlying. The competitive overlap is meaningful: both venues compete for the same pool of on-chain, high-risk-tolerance capital that cycles through DeFi ecosystems.

The timing is notable given Polymarket's already dominant market-share position following the 2024 U.S. election cycle, which drove record volume to the platform. Adding margin deepens user stickiness and raises the stakes — literally — for each contract, potentially accelerating total value locked on the platform as traders pursue amplified returns on political, economic, and sports outcomes.

Why this matters

Margin trading fundamentally changes Polymarket's liquidity profile, making it a more direct competitor to derivatives venues and potentially attracting institutional and semi-professional traders who previously found flat event contracts insufficiently capital-efficient. For prediction-market regulation watchers, leveraged event contracts will sharpen CFTC scrutiny, since margin amplifies both the financial stakes and the systemic-risk arguments regulators use to restrict retail access. Traders on competing platforms like Kalshi should monitor whether margin features become an industry standard that forces a product-arms race.
