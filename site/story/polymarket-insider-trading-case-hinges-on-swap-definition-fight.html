Polymarket Insider Trading Case Hinges on Swap Definition Fight

Published JUL 07, 2026 · 17:27 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Defendants in the CFTC's first-ever event-contract insider trading lawsuit are challenging the case by arguing that Polymarket trades do not legally qualify as swaps under the Commodity Exchange Act.

The CFTC's landmark insider trading action targeting Polymarket activity has hit a foundational legal snag: defense attorneys are contesting whether the prediction-market contracts at issue are 'swaps' as defined under federal commodities law. If the trades fall outside that definition, the CFTC may lack jurisdiction to prosecute insider trading on the platform entirely, potentially gutting the case before it reaches the merits.

The question is not trivial. Swap classification under the Commodity Exchange Act determines whether the CFTC has authority to regulate the instrument, impose anti-fraud rules, and pursue insider trading claims. Polymarket operates on a binary event-contract model that differs structurally from traditional derivatives, and courts have not yet ruled definitively on how such contracts map onto existing statutory categories.

This is the first time the CFTC has brought an insider trading charge specifically tied to event contracts, making the jurisdictional threshold fight especially consequential. The outcome will set precedent for whether federal regulators can police information advantages on prediction platforms like Polymarket and its regulated U.S. competitors such as Kalshi and ForecastEx.

Why this matters

A ruling that Polymarket trades are not swaps would leave a significant enforcement gap, signaling to traders that information advantages on decentralized prediction markets carry little legal risk. Regulated U.S. platforms like Kalshi, whose contracts are explicitly designated, would face a competitive disadvantage if offshore venues operate in a de facto enforcement-free zone. Prediction-market operators and CFTC watchers should track this case closely — the jurisdictional ruling could reshape how event contracts are classified and regulated industry-wide.
