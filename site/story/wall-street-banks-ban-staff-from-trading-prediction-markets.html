Wall Street Banks Ban Staff from Trading Prediction Markets

Published JUL 09, 2026 · 19:57 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Major Wall Street banks have issued internal rules prohibiting employees from trading on prediction markets like Kalshi and Polymarket, citing conflicts of interest and compliance concerns, according to Reuters.

Several large Wall Street institutions have moved to restrict or outright ban staff participation in prediction markets, according to reporting by Reuters and Finextra Research. The crackdowns follow the rapid growth of regulated platforms such as Kalshi and Polymarket, which now offer event contracts on economic data, elections, and financial outcomes that employees at these firms could theoretically influence or trade ahead of.

Banks are treating prediction-market wagers similarly to restrictions already placed on trading individual stocks or options in sectors where employees hold material nonpublic information. Compliance officers are particularly focused on contracts tied to macroeconomic indicators — such as Federal Reserve rate decisions or jobs reports — where analysts and traders may have edge derived from their professional work, according to Finextra Research.

The timing aligns with a broader institutional reckoning around prediction markets as CFTC-regulated platforms expand their product offerings into territory that increasingly overlaps with traditional financial instruments. Kalshi received federal court backing in 2024 to list election contracts, and both it and Polymarket have seen volume surge into the billions of dollars, raising the stakes for how employers manage conflicts.

Why this matters

Bank-level employee bans signal that prediction markets are now consequential enough to threaten compliance frameworks built for traditional securities — a major legitimacy milestone that could accelerate regulatory scrutiny. For platform operators, losing access to a class of highly sophisticated, high-frequency traders may dampen liquidity on macro-linked contracts. Traders watching CFTC rulemaking should note that institutional risk-management departments treating event contracts like securities could strengthen arguments for stricter oversight.
