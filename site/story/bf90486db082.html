Sports Event Contracts Face Legal, Regulatory Pressure on Multiple Fronts

Published APR 21, 2026 · 14:51 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Sports event contracts offered by platforms like Robinhood and Plus500 are drawing simultaneous scrutiny from consumer lawsuits, state legislatures, industry opponents, and former federal regulators.

A consumer lawsuit is now challenging Robinhood's sports event contracts, adding legal risk to a product category that has grown rapidly since the CFTC declined to block similar offerings from Kalshi. Meanwhile, Plus500 announced a U.S. sports event contract launch, signaling continued commercial appetite even as headwinds mount across regulatory and legal channels.

New Jersey legislators have introduced a bill that would require prediction market operators to obtain state licenses before offering sports event contracts to residents, according to Sports Betting Dime. Separately, major gambling industry associations have lobbied the Senate to take action against the contracts, framing them as unlicensed competition to regulated sportsbooks.

A former CFTC commissioner publicly questioned the 'economic purpose' of sports event contracts, per Yahoo Finance and CDC Gaming, arguing the products lack the hedging utility that traditionally justifies CFTC-regulated derivatives. KPMG has also weighed in with analysis on how these contracts should be taxed, reflecting growing mainstream attention to the asset class.

Why this matters

The convergence of a consumer lawsuit, state licensing proposals, and a former regulator's legitimacy challenge creates compounding legal uncertainty that traders should price into any platform-specific positions. If New Jersey's licensing model is adopted by other states, operators face a patchwork compliance burden that could restrict liquidity or force product withdrawals. Prediction market operators expanding into sports contracts — including newer entrants like Plus500 — may face the steepest headwinds as regulatory arbitrage closes.
