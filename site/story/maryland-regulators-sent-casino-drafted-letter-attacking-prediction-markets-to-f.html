Maryland regulators sent casino-drafted letter attacking prediction markets to feds

Published JUL 06, 2026 · 16:40 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Maryland officials submitted a letter to federal regulators opposing prediction markets that was written nearly verbatim by casino industry lobbyists, according to Reason Magazine.

Reason Magazine reported that Maryland copied language crafted by casino lobbyists and forwarded it to federal regulators as the state's own official position against prediction markets. The coordination suggests the gambling industry is actively recruiting state governments to amplify its opposition before federal authorities, potentially the CFTC, which oversees event contracts offered by platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket.

The casino industry has a direct financial stake in blocking prediction markets from expanding into sports and political event contracts, areas where the two industries increasingly compete for the same customer dollars. By ghostwriting state-level regulatory comments, casino lobbies can generate what appears to be broad, independent opposition while masking the commercial motivation behind coordinated submissions.

Reason Magazine's investigation did not name a specific federal docket or deadline, but the pattern mirrors prior industry campaigns during CFTC comment periods on event contracts. Similar astroturfed opposition emerged during Kalshi's multi-year legal battle to offer congressional control markets, which it ultimately won in federal court in 2024.

Why this matters

If casino-drafted language is being laundered through state agencies into federal regulatory records, prediction-market traders should expect a longer, more contentious rulemaking environment at the CFTC. Traders pricing regulatory approval odds on Kalshi or Polymarket should factor in that apparent grassroots opposition may be artificially inflated. Operators and regulation watchers should monitor whether other states submitted similarly drafted letters, which could signal a coordinated multi-state lobbying campaign.
