Traders Sue Polymarket Over Strategy Bitcoin Sale Market Dispute

Published JUL 07, 2026 · 08:34 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

William Wood and Thomas Bush filed suit against Polymarket in New York Supreme Court on July 3, alleging breach of contract after the platform resolved a Strategy bitcoin-sale prediction market as 'No' despite an SEC filing showing Strategy sold 32 BTC between May 26 and May 31.

The complaint, filed in New York Supreme Court, names Polymarket, CEO Shayne Coplan, CMO Matthew Modabber, and related entities as defendants. The plaintiffs held 'Yes' shares in a binary market asking whether Strategy would sell any bitcoin by May, according to The Block. They allege Polymarket wrongly ruled against them even though Strategy's own SEC disclosure confirmed a sale of 32 BTC during the final days of May.

The core legal claims are breach of contract and deceptive practices — a framing that could open Polymarket to damages beyond simple market losses. The disputed resolution hinges on whether Polymarket's resolution criteria adequately captured Strategy's disclosed transaction, a question of market-rule drafting that prediction platforms face constantly. The Block confirmed the filing date as July 3, 2026.

Polymarket has faced mounting scrutiny over how its markets are resolved, particularly when real-world events produce ambiguous or contested outcomes. This case is notable because the factual basis — an SEC filing disclosing a specific BTC sale — is not in dispute; rather, the fight centers on whether Polymarket's resolution logic properly accounted for it.

Why this matters

A successful breach-of-contract ruling against Polymarket would set a precedent that resolution disputes are actionable in court, dramatically raising legal risk for all prediction-market operators. Traders holding positions in markets with subjective or narrowly worded resolution criteria should treat this case as a signal to scrutinize rule language before entering. Regulators watching event-contract platforms will likely cite this litigation as evidence that consumer-protection frameworks are needed.
