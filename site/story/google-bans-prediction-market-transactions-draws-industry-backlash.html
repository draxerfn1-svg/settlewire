Google Bans Prediction Market Transactions, Draws Industry Backlash

Published JUL 08, 2026 · 18:12 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Google has banned transactions related to prediction markets on its platform, a move that has prompted significant pushback from the prediction market industry.

Google's decision to prohibit prediction market transactions marks a significant policy shift that directly affects platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi, which rely on payment rails and app distribution channels controlled by major tech companies. The ban, reported by Yahoo Finance, has triggered immediate criticism from operators and users who argue the restriction is overly broad and commercially damaging.

The backlash reflects growing tension between prediction market platforms — which have gained mainstream legitimacy following the 2024 U.S. election cycle — and gatekeepers like Google, whose payment and advertising policies can effectively determine market reach. Kalshi, for instance, operates as a CFTC-regulated designated contract market, lending the sector a degree of federal legitimacy that critics say Google's policy ignores.

The timing is notable: prediction markets are at a high-water mark of public and regulatory visibility, with the CFTC actively engaged on event-contract rulemaking. A blanket ban by a platform of Google's scale could chill user acquisition and payment processing for even fully regulated operators, raising questions about whether tech-platform policies are becoming a de facto second layer of financial regulation.

Why this matters

Google's ban creates an asymmetric risk for prediction market operators: regulated players like Kalshi bear the same distribution penalty as unregulated ones, undermining the value of CFTC compliance as a competitive moat. Traders should watch whether this accelerates platform migration toward crypto-native venues like Polymarket that are less dependent on Google's payment infrastructure. Regulation watchers should note that if major tech firms act as shadow regulators, formal CFTC frameworks may matter less than Silicon Valley policy decisions.
