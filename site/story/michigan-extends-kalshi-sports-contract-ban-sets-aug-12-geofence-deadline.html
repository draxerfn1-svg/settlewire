Michigan extends Kalshi sports contract ban, sets Aug. 12 geofence deadline

Published JUL 14, 2026 · 09:48 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Michigan regulators have extended their prohibition on Kalshi's sports event contracts and ordered the platform to geofence the state by August 12, giving the company additional time to comply.

Michigan's gaming regulators have pushed back the enforcement deadline for Kalshi's sports-related event contracts, requiring the prediction-market platform to implement geofencing technology that blocks Michigan residents from accessing those markets, according to Gambling Insider. The August 12 cutoff represents an extension from an earlier deadline, signaling that regulators are allowing Kalshi time to build out technical compliance infrastructure rather than pursuing immediate punitive action.

The Michigan Gaming Control Board has been among the most active state regulators scrutinizing Kalshi since the CFTC-regulated platform began offering sports event contracts following a federal court victory that cleared its path to operate nationally. State gambling authorities argue that sports contracts overlap with their jurisdiction, creating a direct clash with federally regulated prediction markets.

Kalshi has faced a patchwork of state-level resistance despite holding CFTC designation as a designated contract market. Several states have issued cease-and-desist orders or demanded geofencing as a compliance measure, forcing the company to navigate conflicting federal and state authority simultaneously.

Why this matters

The Michigan extension signals that state regulators are willing to use deadline pressure as a compliance tool rather than outright bans, a pattern other states may replicate. For prediction-market traders, geofencing creates liquidity fragmentation — markets with fewer eligible participants see wider spreads and less price efficiency. Regulation watchers should track whether Kalshi's CFTC backing ultimately preempts state authority, a question that remains unresolved and has direct implications for the entire event-contract industry.
