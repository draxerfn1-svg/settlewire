CAP urges ban on government insiders trading prediction markets

Published JUL 09, 2026 · 13:02 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

The Center for American Progress published a policy brief calling for prohibitions on government officials trading prediction markets, citing national-security risks from insider-information advantages.

The Center for American Progress (CAP) released a report arguing that federal officials with access to classified or non-public government information pose a unique threat to prediction-market integrity. The think tank contends that such insiders could place profitable event contracts on outcomes they have advance knowledge of, creating asymmetric information advantages unavailable to ordinary market participants.

CAP's brief frames the concern around platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket, which now offer contracts on elections, economic indicators, and geopolitical events — precisely the categories where senior officials routinely hold privileged intelligence. The report urges Congress and regulators to extend or create ethics rules specifically covering event-contract trading, separate from existing stock-trading restrictions such as the STOCK Act.

The policy push arrives as the CFTC has only recently greenlit regulated prediction markets for U.S. audiences, and as scrutiny of who profits from these platforms is intensifying in Washington. CAP's argument mirrors earlier debates over congressional stock trading but extends them into a newer, faster-moving financial instrument with lower entry barriers and higher anonymity potential.

Why this matters

A successful lobbying push for insider-trading rules specific to event contracts could trigger CFTC rulemaking that raises compliance costs and KYC requirements for platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket. Traders should watch whether this narrative gains congressional traction, as new restrictions could narrow liquidity in politically sensitive contract categories. Regulation watchers should note that CAP's framing — national security rather than market fairness — gives the issue bipartisan appeal that could accelerate legislative timelines.
