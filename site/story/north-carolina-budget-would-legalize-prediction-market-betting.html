North Carolina Budget Would Legalize Prediction Market Betting

Published JUL 06, 2026 · 18:02 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

North Carolina's newly proposed state budget includes language that would authorize prediction market betting within the state, marking a significant step toward regulated event-contract wagering at the state level.

According to WRAL, North Carolina's latest budget proposal contains provisions that would explicitly permit prediction market betting, a category distinct from traditional sports wagering. The move signals that state legislators are actively distinguishing between event contracts and standard sports gambling frameworks, rather than treating both under the same regulatory umbrella.

The inclusion comes as platforms like Kalshi and Polymarket have been expanding their footprints across the U.S., with federal-level CFTC oversight already in place for designated contract markets. A state-level authorization in North Carolina would add a new layer of legitimacy, potentially encouraging other states to craft their own explicit prediction-market statutes rather than relying on gray-area interpretations.

WRAL notes the development raises questions about how prediction markets would interact with North Carolina's existing sports betting licensing structure, which went live in 2024. Regulators and operators will need to determine whether separate licensing, taxation, or consumer-protection rules apply specifically to event contracts versus traditional point-spread wagering.

Why this matters

State-level legalization in North Carolina could accelerate a patchwork of state authorizations, complicating the existing federal CFTC framework and creating arbitrage opportunities for platforms already licensed at the federal level. Prediction-market traders should watch whether NC's budget language explicitly names CFTC-regulated contracts, which would affect which platforms can operate immediately. A successful North Carolina model could pressure other large-population states to follow, dramatically expanding the addressable user base for event-contract operators.
