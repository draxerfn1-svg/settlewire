Chicago ban on city employees trading prediction markets on insider info advances

Published JUL 07, 2026 · 17:53 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

A Chicago City Council committee advanced an ordinance that would prohibit municipal employees from using non-public government information to trade on prediction markets, marking one of the first U.S. municipal-level regulations targeting the industry.

According to WTTW News, the Chicago City Council committee moved forward with legislation that would bar city workers from leveraging privileged, non-public information when placing trades on prediction market platforms. The measure represents a direct legislative response to the growing mainstream accessibility of platforms such as Kalshi and Polymarket, which now allow ordinary users to bet on political and policy outcomes.

The ordinance extends existing conflict-of-interest frameworks already applied to traditional financial markets and would apply them specifically to event contracts. City employees with access to sensitive data — such as budget figures, zoning decisions, or public health information — would be explicitly prohibited from using that knowledge to gain an edge on prediction market platforms, according to WTTW News.

The move reflects a broader national conversation about insider-trading risk in prediction markets, a concern that has also surfaced at the federal level as the CFTC weighs expanded event-contract oversight. Chicago's action could serve as a template for other municipalities grappling with the same gap in their ethics codes as prediction markets grow in volume and visibility.

Why this matters

Municipal insider-trading rules targeting prediction markets set a precedent that could accelerate similar ordinances in other major U.S. cities, raising compliance costs for platforms that attract government-employee users. For prediction-market operators, the ordinance signals that regulatory pressure is no longer confined to federal agencies like the CFTC — local governments are now writing the rulebook too. Traders watching political and policy markets should note that tighter information controls on government insiders could, paradoxically, make those markets more efficient by reducing information asymmetry.
