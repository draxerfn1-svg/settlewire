Corporate Crackdown Targets Prediction Markets Amid Explosive Growth

Published JUL 10, 2026 · 19:34 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Prediction markets are experiencing rapid expansion in 2025, but companies and institutions have begun restricting employee participation, signaling a new wave of corporate resistance to the sector, according to Inc.com.

Inc.com reports that prediction markets have surged in visibility and trading volume following high-profile electoral forecasting in 2024, with platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi drawing mainstream attention. That growth has triggered a backlash: employers are beginning to prohibit staff from trading event contracts, citing conflicts of interest and potential misuse of insider information.

The corporate response mirrors early regulatory friction seen in other fintech sectors. Companies with access to sensitive operational or market data are particularly concerned that employees could exploit non-public information to trade event contracts — a compliance gap that existing securities law does not cleanly address, since prediction markets occupy a distinct legal category under CFTC oversight rather than SEC jurisdiction.

The tension highlights a maturing industry grappling with the responsibilities that come with scale. As Kalshi gains federally regulated status for more contract types and Polymarket continues operating via offshore structures, the question of who governs employee conduct on these platforms remains largely unanswered by both regulators and most corporate HR frameworks, according to Inc.com.

Why this matters

Traders should watch whether corporate restrictions create liquidity gaps on specific contract types, particularly those tied to earnings, M&A, or macroeconomic data where information asymmetry is highest. For platform operators, voluntary compliance frameworks — similar to broker-dealer employee trading policies — may become a prerequisite for institutional partnerships and legitimacy. Regulators at the CFTC could use employer concerns as justification to impose stricter participant disclosure rules on designated contract markets.
