Prediction Markets Under Fire for Offering Wildfire Disaster Bets

Published JUL 05, 2026 · 11:30 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Prediction market platforms are drawing sharp public criticism after listing tradeable contracts allowing users to bet on wildfire outcomes, with detractors calling the practice morally reprehensible.

According to Mother Jones, prediction market platforms have begun offering event contracts tied to wildfire disasters, prompting an immediate backlash from critics who argue that profiting from natural catastrophes crosses an ethical line. The outlets reporting on this story have not named specific dollar volumes or contract sizes, but the existence of such markets has itself become a flashpoint.

Mother Jones quotes sources describing the practice as 'morally reprehensible,' reflecting a growing tension between the prediction-market industry's expansion into sensitive real-world events and public expectations around decency. Platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi have previously faced scrutiny for listing contracts on politically charged or tragedy-adjacent topics, and wildfire contracts represent the latest frontier in that ongoing debate.

The wildfire betting controversy arrives as the prediction-market sector is still riding a wave of mainstream attention following high-profile election markets in 2024. Regulators and platform operators alike are now navigating questions about where acceptable market boundaries lie, with each new controversial contract category potentially inviting fresh CFTC scrutiny or congressional attention.

Why this matters

Controversial contract categories like wildfire bets give regulators and legislators a concrete hook to revisit event-contract oversight, raising the probability of new CFTC rulemaking or public hearings. Traders in political and policy prediction markets should watch for sentiment spillover — backlash against one platform can tighten the regulatory environment for the entire sector. Operators considering disaster-linked contracts must now weigh reputational risk as a material factor alongside liquidity.
