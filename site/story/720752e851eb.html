Thin Markets and Bots Plague Most Prediction Contract Listings

Published JUL 02, 2026 · 12:47 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

The vast majority of prediction market contracts trade at low volumes, exposing users to price manipulation, erratic swings, and automated trading exploitation, according to CNBC.

Most contracts listed on prediction market platforms see little to no meaningful trading activity, CNBC reports, creating a landscape where a handful of high-profile markets — presidential elections, major economic indicators — attract the bulk of liquidity while hundreds of others languish with minimal participation.

According to CNBC, the thin order books on low-volume contracts leave retail traders vulnerable to sharp price dislocations that may not reflect genuine probability shifts. Bots, which can place and cancel orders rapidly, are able to dominate these illiquid markets in ways that would be harder to sustain on deeper books, effectively setting artificial prices.

The issue is not unique to any single platform but reflects a structural challenge across the prediction market industry. Platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi have grown rapidly in public profile, yet that visibility has not translated into broad liquidity distribution across their full contract catalogs, leaving the majority of listed events thinly traded.

Why this matters

For traders, low-volume contracts carry hidden execution risk — the spread between bid and ask can dwarf any informational edge, and bot activity can front-run retail orders. For platform operators, the liquidity concentration problem undermines the core utility argument for prediction markets and may invite regulatory scrutiny over whether thinly traded contracts constitute fair markets. Regulators at the CFTC, already watchful over event contract design, could use evidence of bot dominance in illiquid markets to tighten listing standards.
