Chicago eyes ban on Kalshi, Polymarket trading by city insiders

Published JUL 07, 2026 · 18:50 UTC · SettleWire newsroom

Chicago is considering a prohibition on city employees with access to nonpublic information from trading on prediction markets like Kalshi and Polymarket, according to the Chicago Tribune.

Chicago officials are weighing new ethics rules that would bar municipal employees who hold privileged government information from placing event-contract wagers on platforms including Kalshi and Polymarket, the Chicago Tribune reported. The proposal targets insiders who could theoretically exploit advance knowledge of city decisions — such as budget announcements, zoning rulings, or public-health declarations — to profit on outcome markets.

The move reflects growing awareness among local governments that prediction markets have matured into liquid venues where nonpublic information could generate real financial gains. Kalshi, which won federal court approval in 2024 to offer political event contracts, and Polymarket, a crypto-based platform processing hundreds of millions in monthly volume, are specifically named in the Chicago deliberations as platforms of concern.

No formal ordinance text had been publicly released as of the Tribune's coverage, meaning the proposal remains in an early deliberative stage. Still, the discussion signals that municipal regulators are beginning to craft insider-trading-style frameworks specifically tailored to prediction markets, a governance gap that federal oversight has yet to fully address.

Why this matters

A Chicago ban would establish one of the first municipal insider-trading precedents explicitly naming prediction-market platforms, potentially inspiring copycat ordinances in other major cities. For Kalshi and Polymarket, named-platform prohibitions in government ethics codes add reputational and regulatory friction even without federal action. Traders pricing political and policy contracts should watch whether such rules eventually expand to state employees, who often have earlier access to market-moving information.
